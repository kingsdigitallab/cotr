Introduction

This is an experimental prototype visualisation of the unsettled regions.

It tries to help answer the following questions in a visual and intuitive way:

  1. Outlier detection: which text disrupts the version or the work the most in a given area? I.e. who's the 'troublemaker'?
  2. Heterogeneity: how much do the texts disagree with each other in a given area?
  3. Clustering: which group of texts tend to agree with each other in a given area?

The heatmap visualisation is similar in the sense that it shows the spatial distribution of heterogeneity (question 2). However since this visualisation shows the selected texts in parallel, it enables comparative analysis.

This tabular representation is quite common to represent collations. Each column is a text and each row represent an unsettled region. The regions appear in the sequential order of the text. Each cell is the reading for a particular text in a given region

What do the colors mean?

In a row, the cells with identical readings will have identical colors. The most represented readings will be green and the less represented will be blue, with shades within that spectrum for intermediate groups. Also, the more a reading differ from the other in the region the more red it looks.

Unlike other established conventions with tabular visualisations of collations, green and red don't translate into 'correct reading' and a mistake. In line with the principles of a dynamic edition the colors are not influenced by a specific text, they encourage you to explore all alternatives impartially.

Limitations & caveats

The coloring and underlying measures of similarity are full of caveats as there are imperfect and arbitrary. The visualisation is a tool to intuitively explore the dynamic of the edition through the distributions of differences but no conclusion should be drawn from it without careful textual analysis. It's aim is to draw attentions to variations and patterns that might be easily missed when looking at a couple of manuscript folios side by side.

The difference is measured using difflib in Python: it looks at the number of characters in common between two readings. It is purely syntactic, with no concept of semantic. So magnificus and malificus would be considered almost identical but uel and aut very different.

Geoffroy Noel
